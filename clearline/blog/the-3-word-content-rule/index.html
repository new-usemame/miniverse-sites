The 3-word content rule that converts

When your message feels vague, don’t add more words. Build it around the three things a buyer needs to believe: problem, promise, and proof.

C

Clearline ContentPractical writing advice for growing teams · July 12, 2026

Most business content asks readers to connect too many dots. It describes a company, lists features, and hopes the value becomes obvious.

But a busy founder or buyer is making a faster decision: “Is this for me, will it help, and can I trust it?” If your copy cannot answer those questions quickly, polish will not save it.

A simple framework can: problem, promise, proof.

1. Problem: show that you understand the moment

Start with the friction your customer already feels—not your category, company history, or newest feature. A precise problem makes the right reader feel recognized.

Before

“An all-in-one collaboration platform for modern teams.”

After

“Stop losing client approvals across email, chat, and spreadsheets.”

The second version names a situation. Readers who live with that situation immediately know the message is relevant.

2. Promise: make the better outcome concrete

Your promise is the useful change you help create. It should be specific enough to picture and credible enough to believe.

“Transform your workflow” is broad. “Collect every client comment and approval in one place” describes what becomes easier. Good promises reduce uncertainty instead of inflating ambition.

A strong promise tells the customer what gets better—not how impressive the company wants to sound.

3. Proof: give belief a reason

Problem and promise earn attention. Proof earns trust. Use the strongest evidence you can honestly support:

  • A specific customer result or attributed testimonial
  • A short product demonstration or before-and-after example
  • A transparent process, guarantee, or turnaround time
  • Relevant customer volume, experience, or credentials

Early-stage businesses may not have a library of case studies yet. That is fine. Show the work: explain the process, publish a useful sample, or make a small promise you can reliably keep. Never invent proof to fill the space.

Put the rule to work on your homepage

Your first screen does not need to contain every detail, but it should establish the sequence:

  1. Problem: Name the costly or frustrating situation.
  2. Promise: State the clear outcome you create.
  3. Proof: Add one credible reason to believe you.

For example: “Still chasing client feedback across five tools? Bring every comment and approval into one review link. Trusted for 12,000 creative projects.”

The framework also works for emails, sales pages, and founder posts. The proportions can change, but the logic remains: recognition, outcome, belief.

A five-minute editing test

Open one important page and highlight the sentence that expresses each word. Then ask:

  • Is the problem specific to a real customer moment?
  • Can a reader picture the promise?
  • Is the proof relevant, honest, and close to the claim?

If one color is missing, you have found the gap. Fix that before adding another section. Clear content is rarely about saying more; it is about making the right three ideas impossible to miss.
